## The four voice pillars

These come from the official Chamelo brand book and apply to every piece of copy.

### 1. Confident and Knowledgeable

We know our craft inside and out. Our tone is authoritative and bold yet approachable, making complex technology easy to understand. We communicate with clarity and assurance. Our confidence instills trust.

In practice:
- Lead with what we know, not what we hope
- Skip hedging language ("maybe," "kind of," "we think")
- Make claims specific: "0.1 seconds," "63% to 17% VLT," not "fast" or "wide range"

### 2. Fun and Young-at-Heart

Chamelo brings lively, youthful energy to every experience. Our tone is light-hearted, playful, and vibrant. We're not afraid to use an exclamation mark or sprinkle in humor when it fits the moment.

In practice:
- A well-placed exclamation point is fine. Two in a row isn't.
- Humor is permitted, but only when it lands — when in doubt, cut it
- Pattern-interrupt copy ("Sunglasses, but they listen.") wins on social

### 3. Passionate and Personal

We love what we do, and it shows. Chamelo's tone is warm, engaging, and personal. We're here to connect with people, not just sell eyewear. Leave the corporate jargon behind — use real names, remember details, add a thoughtful human touch.

In practice:
- Talk like you're catching up with a friend — genuine, excited, approachable
- Use real customer names when quoting reviews
- Avoid corporate jargon and PR-speak

### 4. Keep It Real

Authentic, sincere, straightforward. We embrace boldness and are unafraid to speak our mind, always with integrity. "Keeping it real" means no fluff, no BS, just authenticity. Same energy as Stephon Marbury.

In practice:
- Don't oversell. The product does the talking.
- Acknowledge limitations when they're relevant ("Not polarized — for that, see Dusk Classic")
- Founder voice is real voice — let Marbury sound like Marbury, not like a script

## Sentence structures that work for Chamelo

### Short declarative triples
- *"Four colors. One tap. Instant calm."*
- *"Tint Control. Audio. Knicks."*
- *"Built for miles, not couch time."*
- *"Run light. Hear everything."*

### Benefit-first, feature-second
- *"Adjust lens tint in 0.1 seconds"* not *"Eclipse™ electrochromic lens technology"*
- *"From bright to blackout in 0.1s"* not *"63% to 17% VLT range"*

### Real-world problem → solution
- *"Going in and out of tunnels used to mean swapping glasses constantly. Now I just slide and go."*
- *"From city chaos to instant calm in 0.1 seconds."*

### One frame, many uses
- *"One frame. Four vibes."*
- *"One frame. Long session."*
- *"One frame for every version of you."*

### Action verbs as openers
- Run, Tap, Slide, Lock, Hear, See, Stay, Built, Train, Made

## What Chamelo never sounds like

### Banned phrases (do not use)
- "Game-changing" (overused — we are one, we don't say it)
- "Revolutionary"
- "Unleash" anything
- "Next-level"
- "Push your limits"
- "Go further"
- "Take it to the next level"
- "Disrupting [category]"
- "World-class" (when not literal)
- "Best in class" (when not measurable)

### Tones to avoid
- Generic sport brand clichés ("push your limits", "go further", "elevate your game")
- Discount or clearance language that undermines premium positioning ("crazy deal," "must-go," "while supplies last" — use scarcity language sparingly and only when factually true)
- Overly technical spec dumps without context
- Apologetic or hedging language
- Try-hard punchlines that mimic our short-form style but feel forced ("One button. Three tints. Zero excuses.")
- Luxury-adjacent clichés ("crafted for the discerning," "elevated essentials")

### A note on AI-generated content

Our official social strategy says: "Avoid AI-generated voices, videos, and photos as much as possible — prioritize real-life, authentic content."

Our practical position for paid creative in 2026:

AI-generated content is permitted and expected as part of our creative pipeline. But every AI-generated asset must pass for authentic at first glance. That means:

- Soul ID training is required for any AI-generated human likeness (no generic AI faces)
- Figma finishing is required for any AI-generated visual (no raw Higgsfield outputs)
- AI-generated voices are not used in paid ads — real ambassadors, real voiceover, real customers only
- Every AI-generated ad carries Meta's AI Content Label per policy

If an AI-generated asset looks AI, it doesn't ship. The bar is "indistinguishable from production photography," not "good enough for AI."

## Examples of approved copy in context

These examples show the voice working in different formats. Pull from these patterns when writing.

### Aura — the mood-changing positioning (the strongest Aura voice we have)

The thing Aura actually does for the wearer isn't style — it's mood. Change the color, change the state. Every strong Aura piece leads with that.

*"Your mood, rendered in color."*

Why it works: four words, evocative imagery, customer-emotional. Doesn't say what the product does — says what it does FOR THE PERSON. The mechanism is implied.

*"From city chaos to instant calm in 0.1 seconds."*

Why it works: real situation → real benefit → real spec, in one line. Sounds like someone describing the actual experience, not selling. The 0.1 seconds isn't a feature stat here — it's the punchline.

*"The first sunglasses that match your energy."*

Why it works: category-defining ("first") + customer-emotional (your energy) + confident without overselling. The product is the supporting character.

*"Reset your mood in a tap."*

Why it works: command form, single benefit, no qualifiers. Tells the customer what they get, not what we built.

### Aura — Calm vs Energy framing

The lens palette choice is the mood choice. Frame it as a state, not a color.

*"Energy stimulates energy and happiness. Calm is soothing and reset. Tap to choose."*

Why it works: simple cause/effect, lets the customer self-select the state they want, treats the lens choice as identity not aesthetics.

### Music Shield Gen 2 — the strongest hooks

*"Music stays. Glasses stay. You go."*

Why it works: three-beat rhythm, no-friction framing, sounds like a friend telling you about their commute. The "you go" lands because the first two beats set up the stability — nothing in your way.

*"Train harder. Carry less."*

Why it works: clean paradox — usually more = more, here less = more. Two beats, command form, athletic register without sport clichés.

*"Run light. Hear everything."*

Why it works: four words, full product story. "Light" covers the frame weight. "Hear everything" covers the open-ear audio that doesn't block the world out. Nothing wasted.

*"Audio on. Head up."*

Why it works: rhythm + posture. Captures the safety/awareness benefit through behavior, not a feature claim. The customer can picture themselves doing it.

### Music Shield Gen 2 — situational benefit framing

*"Going in and out of tunnels used to mean swapping glasses constantly. Now I just slide and go."*

Why it works: real customer language (verbatim from Kevin Lim review). Names a frustration the customer didn't know they had a name for, then resolves it in seven words. This is the model for problem-solution copy in Chamelo voice — never invent the friction, pull it from real customer language.

### Optional side piece, not a hero reference

*"The next gen of our next-gen tint-adjustable audio sunglasses."*

This is a fun self-aware short description that plays the joke on Gen 2. Funny works in the right context (PDP, social caption). Don't treat this as a main reference for the voice — it's a side piece, not a lead.

## What makes a good Chamelo line, distilled

Looking at the examples above, the pattern that wins:

- **Lead with the wearer's state**, not the product feature. ("Your mood, rendered in color." not "Four-color Prismatic™ technology.")
- **Short over clever.** Three to seven words beats a witty paragraph.
- **Triples and command forms.** "Run light. Hear everything." / "Music stays. Glasses stay. You go." Rhythm is part of the brand.
- **Real moments over abstract benefits.** "From city chaos to instant calm" lands because the customer has felt city chaos.
- **The product is the supporting character.** Mood, music, run, calm — the customer is the lead.
- **No qualifiers.** Cut "really," "truly," "amazingly," "literally." If the claim needs a qualifier, the claim is weak.

## When in doubt

If you can't decide between two phrasings, ask:

1. Would Marbury say this out loud? (Keep-it-real test)
2. Does this lead with the benefit or the feature? (Choose benefit)
3. Could a competitor have written this same line? (If yes, rewrite)
4. Is there a banned word in it? (If yes, replace)
5. Would a 14-year-old understand it? (If no, simplify)
