## What it is

Aura is the only pair of sunglasses that change your mood.

The lenses switch between four colors with a touch — but the real product isn't the color change. It's what the color change does for the person wearing them. Warm tones (Energy) push the wearer toward alert, bright, happy. Cool tones (Calm) shift them toward calm, soothed, reset. The customer isn't buying four lens colors. They're buying control over their state.

The world's first touch-controlled color-changing sunglasses. The first sunglasses that match — and shift — your energy.

---

## Core positioning: mood, not style

Every strong Aura piece leads with the wearer's state, not the product feature. The mechanism is implied. The benefit is emotional.

What Aura does for the customer:
- Resets your mood in a tap
- Takes you from city chaos to instant calm
- Switches your energy with your outfit
- Gives you control over how you feel, not just how you look

What Aura is not:
- A color-coordination accessory
- A style/fashion play (style is a side effect)
- A performance/sport product
- A "smart glasses" tech showcase

If the copy could equally describe a regular pair of sunglasses with four interchangeable lens tints, the copy is wrong. The point is mood.

---

## The psychology behind Calm vs Energy

This is the why behind the lens palette choice. Use this in voice notes, longer-form copy, and when the customer needs to feel the difference.

**Energy** (warm tones — Clear, Yellow, Pink, Orange) stimulates energy and happiness. The boardroom-to-beach palette. The "power up" mode. Choose Energy when you want your daily default to be brighter and more alert.

**Calm** (cool tones — Crystal, Indigo, Ruby, Violet) is soothing and reset. The chaos-to-calm palette. The "wind down" mode. Choose Calm when you want your daily default to be lower, cooler, more contained.

Customers self-select into the palette that matches their default. The lens palette is identity — not a feature.

## How Aura works as a product

Aura is built around four independent customer choices:

1. **Frame color** — Glossy Black or White
2. **Frame model** — currently Aura. More Prismatic frames are planned.
3. **Lens palette** — Calm or Energy (each is a set of four colors that cycle on tap)
4. **Audio** — with or without built-in Bluetooth audio

This produces 8 active SKUs today (2 colors × 2 lens palettes × audio or no audio) from one frame model. The structure scales as we add new Prismatic-compatible frames and lens palettes.

### Choice ladder for PDP and configurator

Sequence the configurator top-to-bottom by visibility commitment:
**Color → Lens palette → Audio**

Frame color is the most visible/personal choice (everyone sees it). Lens palette is the mood layer. Audio is the upgrade.

### Naming in copy

- **Default reference: "Aura."** The lens palette is a feature you highlight, not a separate product name.
- When the lens palette is the point of the message: "Aura with Energy lenses," "Aura with Calm lenses," or "Aura Calm" / "Aura Energy" as shorthand.
- Use **"Aura Audio"** only when audio is the relevant differentiator.
- When the choice itself is the hook: "Pick your color. Pick your lens. Pick your sound."

## Voice and positioning notes

- **Mood is the lead. Color is the mechanism. Style is the side effect.** If a piece of copy doesn't make the reader feel like they could shift their state by tapping, it's not Aura voice.
- Don't borrow performance/sport language from Music Shield. Aura is mood, not workout.
- Don't lean on luxury-fashion clichés ("elevated essentials," "refined sophistication"). Aura is more emotional than that.
- Pull customer language from how they actually describe the experience: "matches my mood," "shifts how I feel," "instant calm," "wakes me up."
- The 0.1 seconds spec lands as a punchline (after the mood moment), not as a feature in itself.
- Calm vs Energy is identity language. Treat the palette choice as the customer telling you who they are by default.
- Audio is a supporting upgrade. Don't lead with it. Lead with mood, mention the sound.
- Avoid: "performance," "stay locked in," "built for [sport]," "elevate your," "luxury," "premium" used in isolation.
